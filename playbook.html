01 The rules of the room

Announced in S0, enforced all day. These rules ARE the safe space the poster promises.

No laptops, one exception

Laptops stay closed and phones stay away all day, except the ten minutes of S2 when everyone meets the employee on their own phone. Paper does the rest. "Today runs on paper and conversation. You will use your phone exactly once, and I will tell you when."

Q&A: 1 to 3 per segment

Every segment ends with room for one to three questions. Anything bigger gets parked with one fixed line: "That is bigger than today. It goes on the Later List, and it is yours at the end." Producer writes it on the flip chart verbatim.

The no-judgment contract

Opened with the poll and the stat: 91% of C-suite executives admit to pretending to know more about AI than they do (Pluralsight, 2025). "If I use a technical word without decoding it, you are allowed to boo."

The Script · beat sheets, verbatim moments, and your story slots

Three tiers of scripting. Verbatim (red-bar blocks): deliver word for word until it's yours; these carry the promises, the activities, and the close. Beat sheets: the sequence and the key lines; your phrasing. Story cards: industry stories with their setup, turn, and lesson; dashed red boxes mark where your personal stories replace or join them. Rehearse the whole thing with a timer at least five times; the first live run always goes 20% long.

JOBThe collective exhale: "you are not behind, you are normal." Engineered relief is the emotional peak of the afternoon.

A18 · The Vendor Game (P7 ×3)S9 · 9 MIN · THE SLEEPER HIT · NEVER CUT BELOW 2 CLAIMS
JOBBuilds the nose. The block attendees retell to peers, which makes it the referral engine.
SPECThree anonymized claims from real pitches, one at a time: show, vote real-or-fluff, reveal the split, dissect (the weasel word, the exposing question, the honest version). Keep a running score with the room ("the room is 2 for 2, last one's the hard one").
WRONGSomeone recognizes a vendor: "no names in this room, and if you recognized it, that tells you how common the wording is." Room gets all three right: "congratulations, you cannot be sold to anymore; the scorecard in your hands is for your team, who still can."
